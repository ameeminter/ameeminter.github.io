UX Strategy · App Design · Wireframing

GigBee: Gig Worker Earnings Tracker

As an eight-year participant in the gig economy — driving rideshare, completing usability tests, and attending focus groups — I knew firsthand that no existing app could track the full picture of modern gig work. I designed GigBee to fill that gap.

Framework

Garrett's 5 Elements of UX

Tools

Figma, Adobe Illustrator, MS Word

Deliverables

Lo-fi & hi-fi wireframes, UX strategy report

Problem & Process

  • Existing apps (Stride, Hurdlr) either lacked customization or created excessive cognitive load through invasive permissions and complex dashboards
  • Gig workers doing non-driving work — focus groups, usability tests, mystery shopping — had no way to track those earnings alongside traditional gig income
  • Designed for "gigtrics": a dashboard-first approach where users can log income, mileage, and expenses in seconds without friction
  • Used the strategy, scope, structure, skeleton, and surface planes to move systematically from user needs to a polished hi-fi prototype

Methods & Tools

Competitive Analysis · Wireframing · Hi-Fi Prototype · Information Architecture · Figma · Adobe Illustrator

02

Moderated Usability Test · Research Report

Can Residents Find Help on Maricopa.gov?

With homelessness and inflation rising in Arizona, accessing human services quickly can be a matter of safety. I conducted an in-person moderated usability test to determine whether Maricopa County residents could locate food assistance, mental health resources, job training, and rent help on the county's official website.

Method

Moderated in-person usability test

Participants

3 participants (ages 16, 41, 73)

Tasks

4 human services tasks

Key Findings

  • None of the three participants completed all four tasks — revealing a critical accessibility gap in a site serving 4.5 million residents
  • All three participants said they would use the search bar, yet the site buries it in the upper right corner and provides no instruction to use it
  • Related human services were scattered across departments, causing participants to revisit previously-found paths without success
  • A page linking all human services existed but required a 5-click path to reach: Departments → Human Services → Apply for Help → Other Assistance Programs → All Services

Recommendations

  • Move the search bar to the center of the homepage with instructional helper text
  • Consolidate all human services under a single, easily-reached landing page
  • Add a "GET HELP WITH..." category to the main navigation with comprehensive subheadings

Methods & Tools

Think-Aloud Protocol · Entry & Exit Questionnaires · Task Analysis · Audio Recording · Steve Krug's Rocket Surgery

Community Website · Accessibility

Solera HOA

03

Moderated Usability Test · Accessibility Research

Usability & Accessibility in a 55+ Community

A gated retirement community's HOA website was supposed to serve its residents — but its design made even routine tasks like paying fees and submitting forms unnecessarily difficult. I tested the site with three retired residents (ages 73–83) to understand why, and to recommend practical fixes.

Method

Moderated in-person usability test

Participants

3 participants (ages 73, 83, 83)

Tasks

Login, e-form submission, HOA payment

Key Findings

  • The login mechanism was hidden inside a large hero image — the most frequent user action was inaccessible to first-time visitors
  • An "E-Forms" page existed under Contacts navigation but contained no actual forms, sending users on a dead-end journey
  • The third-party payment portal looked unofficial to participants; one noted the site name (PayLease) didn't match the actual site (Zego) — a trust and security issue
  • All three participants raised accessibility concerns: font size, contrast, and readability were insufficient for the site's core demographic

Recommendations

  • Replace the hidden login with a clear, centered login form above the fold
  • Reorganize navigation using card sorting; separate community social features from HOA administrative functions
  • Make e-forms submittable online to accommodate residents with mobility limitations
  • Add an accessibility widget on the login page, prominently displayed

Methods & Tools

Think-Aloud Protocol · Post-Test Survey · Accessibility Evaluation · Task Analysis · Steve Krug's Rocket Surgery

About Me

From a cupholder
to a career path.

My path to UX started with a badly designed cupholder. During a period when my high-mileage car spent more time in the shop than on the road, I was renting vehicles constantly. Each rental was a tour of someone's design decisions — some delightful, most frustrating. The turning point was a cupholder positioned so that selecting the correct gear required navigating around a water bottle. I wanted to understand why that happened and how to prevent it.

"I realized the questions I'd been asking since childhood were the same ones UX designers ask every day."

That realization led me to enroll in Arizona State University's B.S. Technical Communication — User Experience program, which I completed in August 2024 with a 4.0 GPA.

Beyond coursework, I've participated in 350+ moderated and unmoderated usability tests as a paid participant across multiple research firms. I don't just show up for the compensation — I treat each session as fieldwork. I observe how facilitators structure tasks and handle confusion, notice which interfaces cause friction and which ones don't. Im able to observe the differences between poorly run and well-run test. This perspective from the participant's side has shaped how I think about test design in my own research.

I'm currently deepening my skill set through OOUX (Object-Oriented UX) via Sophia Prater's video training and podcast, The UX Level-Up. I'm also building a quantitative research foundation, having earned a 99.4% in Elements of Statistics (STP226) at ASU. Comfort with data — not just qualitative insights — is something I want to bring to every project.
